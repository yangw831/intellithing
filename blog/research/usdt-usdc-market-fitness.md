# USDT and USDC: Two Different Kinds of Market Fitness

Research brief for article #2  
Research date: June 27, 2026  
Status: Primary-source research complete; article not yet drafted

## Working thesis

USDT and USDC should not be evaluated on a single leaderboard.

- **USDT demonstrates distribution-led fitness:** substantially larger circulation,
  broad exchange and wallet availability, established cross-border use, and a strong
  position where access to digital dollars matters more than integration with a
  regulated U.S. financial workflow.
- **USDC demonstrates institution- and integration-led fitness:** a reserve structure
  designed around cash and short-duration government instruments, public-company
  reporting, native developer infrastructure, and visible adoption in regulated
  payment and settlement programs.

The article should not declare a universal winner. It should ask: **fit for which
market, user, counterparty, and job?**

## Evidence snapshot

### 1. Circulation shows different scale, not complete market fitness

At March 31, 2026:

- Tether reported approximately **$183 billion** of token-related liabilities.
- Circle reported **$77.049 billion** of USDC in circulation.
- Circle's average USDC in circulation increased 39.1% year over year in Q1 2026.

This establishes that USDT remained much larger by outstanding supply at the same
reporting date, while USDC was growing quickly. Outstanding supply is evidence of
adoption and dollar demand, but it is not equivalent to payment volume, unique users,
liquidity in every venue, or suitability for a specific workflow.

### 2. USDT's strongest case is reach and existing distribution

Tether describes USD₮ use across exchanges, payments, wallets, infrastructure,
remittances, savings, and trading. Its current support documentation lists issuance
or integration paths across networks including Ethereum, Tron, Solana, TON, Aptos,
Avalanche, Celo, Kaia, Liquid, Polkadot, and Tezos.

Tether reported more than 570 million users as of March 2026. That is an
issuer-reported figure and should be attributed as such rather than presented as an
independently verified count.

Tether's own Q4 2025 market report also distinguishes among savings, centralized
exchange, sender, and DeFi wallet behavior. The useful editorial point is that
USDT's market fitness is not reducible to crypto trading; however, the behavioral
figures in that report rely partly on Chainalysis and Artemis data and should be
described transparently.

### 3. USDC's strongest case is institutional and developer integration

Circle reported that USDC was natively integrated on 30 chains by the end of 2025.
Circle Mint and its APIs provide controlled minting, redemption, and chain support
for institutions and developers.

The institutional case is visible outside Circle:

- Visa launched USDC settlement for select U.S. issuer and acquirer partners in
  December 2025. Visa said the program provides seven-day settlement availability
  and reported stablecoin settlement volume above a $3.5 billion annualized run rate
  as of November 30, 2025.
- Circle launched CPN Managed Payments in April 2026 so payment providers, banks,
  fintechs, and enterprises can use stablecoin settlement while Circle manages the
  digital-asset lifecycle and compliance controls.

### 4. Reserve quality and assurance should be compared precisely

Both issuers report reserves exceeding token liabilities, but their reserve
composition and assurance models differ.

Tether reported at March 31, 2026:

- Approximately $183 billion of token-related liabilities.
- $8.23 billion of excess reserves.
- Approximately $141 billion of direct and indirect U.S. Treasury-bill exposure.
- Additional reserve assets including physical gold and bitcoin.
- A quarterly attestation prepared by BDO.

Tether announced in March 2026 that it had engaged a Big Four firm for its first
full financial-statement audit. Unless that audit is published before the article
goes live, the article must clearly distinguish a reserve attestation from a
completed financial-statement audit.

Circle says:

- USDC reserves are limited to cash and the Circle Reserve Fund.
- The Circle Reserve Fund is an SEC-registered Rule 2a-7 government money-market
  fund managed by BlackRock.
- Reserve holdings and mint/burn flows are disclosed weekly.
- A Big Four accounting firm provides monthly assurance.
- Circle also publishes audited financial statements and quarterly filings as a
  U.S. public company.

This is not simply a "backed or unbacked" comparison. It is a comparison of asset
composition, liquidity design, disclosure frequency, assurance scope, and
institutional accountability.

## Proposed article structure

1. **The wrong question: which stablecoin is better?**
   Introduce fitness by job rather than a universal ranking.
2. **Two products called digital dollars**
   Explain the shared 1:1 dollar objective and the very different distribution
   systems around it.
3. **USDT: distribution is a product feature**
   Circulation, geographic reach, exchange/wallet availability, and cross-border
   dollar access.
4. **USDC: integration is a product feature**
   Reserve design, public reporting, APIs, chain infrastructure, and institutional
   payment programs.
5. **Trust is not one variable**
   Separate price stability, redeemability, reserve liquidity, disclosure,
   regulatory fit, counterparty access, and operational reliability.
6. **A fitness matrix**
   Compare likely fit for trading liquidity, cross-border dollar access, remittance,
   institutional settlement, enterprise treasury, merchant payments, and agentic
   commerce.
7. **What builders should ask before choosing**
   Corridor, user wallet, chain, on/off-ramp, redemption access, compliance,
   transaction cost, smart-contract support, and counterparty acceptance.
8. **Conclusion: the market is selecting for different strengths**
   USDT's scale does not make USDC irrelevant; USDC's institutional design does not
   erase USDT's distribution advantage.

## Proposed fitness matrix

These are hypotheses to argue carefully, not unsupported scores.

| Job | Likely advantage | Reason to test |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Crypto exchange liquidity | USDT | Existing exchange and trading distribution |
| Cross-border digital-dollar access | USDT | Reach, wallets, and emerging-market use |
| U.S. institutional settlement | USDC | Public reporting and visible payment-network integration |
| Enterprise/API integration | USDC | Circle Mint, APIs, CPN, and native chain infrastructure |
| Consumer remittance | Corridor-dependent | Wallet, off-ramp, chain fee, and local acceptance dominate |
| Merchant payments | Context-dependent | Acceptance, settlement preference, and PSP integration matter |
| Agentic commerce | Too early for one winner | Compliance, programmability, wallet support, and merchant settlement all matter |

## Editorial safeguards

- Date every circulation, reserve, user, chain, and volume figure.
- Attribute issuer-reported adoption figures to the issuer.
- Do not use market capitalization as a synonym for transaction volume.
- Do not use onchain transfer volume as a synonym for economic payment volume.
- Distinguish native issuance from bridged versions of a stablecoin.
- Distinguish attestations from financial-statement audits.
- Explain that direct redemption access and terms can differ from secondary-market
  access.
- Avoid treating regulatory posture as a moral score; analyze the practical effect
  on a specific use case.
- Recheck all facts immediately before publication.

## Evidence still needed for the full draft

Primary sources establish the issuers' reserve models, circulation, supported
networks, and named institutional integrations. A rigorous claim about aggregate
trading liquidity, geographic usage, or chain-level transaction behavior would
require venue data or a reputable aggregated onchain dataset. If used, that evidence
should be labeled as secondary research rather than quietly mixed with issuer data.
